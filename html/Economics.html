Immigration and Aging In The EU
Immigration and Europe

Europe faced a wave of migration during the European Migrant Crisis, and now, the War in Ukraine.
Countries that accepted many refugees are some of the oldest countries in the world by average population age: Germany, Italy, Greece and Spain etc.
Eastern European countries like Latvia and Lithuania, and Romania are expected to see the largest reductions in population by 2100, as much as 43.7% for Latvia [1]
Immigration has served as a means of replenishing workforce in host countries,
however, their labor market performance vary widely depending on their education, and reason for migration.

Issues an aging society faces

Many European nations rely on income and consumption taxes to fund the extensive welfare programmes designed to help its citizens.
With an aging population, the number in the workforce will shrink, and dependent population will rise (those younger than 15, older than 65)
A shrinking working population results in lower tax revenue, thus, reducing the ability of a nation to fund welfare programmes in a sustainable manner.
It is tempting to incentivize people to have children, but these children will not begin working for at least another 15 years.

Immigration offers a temorary solution, if done correctly

In this paper, I attempt to answer the question about immigration status, and education in the European Union, using microdata from EQLS 2016,
with information on household income, EU-birth status, immigration status, and education status.

Issue with original paper: Did not account for survey weights

Original paper did not take survey weights into consideration, thus, results are inaccurate.
Paper is currently being revised to take survey weights into account.
