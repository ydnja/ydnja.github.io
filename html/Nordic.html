Nordic Model, A Comparative Analysis Between Norway and Finland
Short History of modern Norway and Finland's welfare policies
Finland

Like the rest of the continent, war torn Finland saw nationalization of many production, yet, no company was nationalized.
The post-war Communist-Social Democratic coalition contributed to a largely planned economy.
Modern Finnish welfare state began in the 50s, with poor relief policies, and pension reform in 1956.
Healthcare was reformed in 1964 with the inclusion of maternity allowance. Healthcare was made universal in 1972.
After the recession of early 1990s, Finland saw a phase of liberalization in the subsequent recovery,
eventually forming the modern Finnish welfare state, we see today.

Norway

Postwar Norway saw government centralization of many production industries under Labor Party
Prime Einar Gerhardsen, whose government also presided over the creation a pension system and unemployment support.
Various other types of welfare policies, including child and disability, were also adopted in late 1940s and 1950s.
These welfare policies weren't just passed under the center-left Labor government, but also under center
right coalitions, which passed the National Social Security Reform in the late 1960s.
After the halt of postwar expansion in early 1970s, Norwegian economy saw a wave of liberalization in the
1980s and 1990s like the rest of the western world, creating the current, "pro-business" nordic model we see today.

Fiscal Sustainability Outlook In Relation to Aging
Finland

Welfare programmes are funded largely through taxation, mainly income and consumption taxes.
Finland's marginal income tax rate stands at almost 60%, and a 24% VAT.[8] Therefore increasing income and consumption taxes is not feasible.
Yet, neither is cutting welfare programmes, as it is considered a political suicide.
A potential solution is immigration, however, immigrant integration is not an easy task and it remains a controversial topic for many.
Unlike Norway, Finland is not gifted with natural resources, and its population is expected to shrink beginning 2031,
Therefore, there is a sense of urgency to find a feasible solution.

Norway

Unlike Finland, Norway is not expected to lose population until beyond 2100, thanks to slow-falling fertility rates.
Norwegians also enjoy slightly lower marginal tax rates (around 50%) than their Finnish neighbors,[9]
potentially leaving the government with room to adjust. Norway is also gifted with natural resources in the Norwegian continental shelf,
and its mountainous landscape allows it to leverage hydro-electricity to meet its energy demands.
Norway could observe policy consequences of faster-aging nations and learn from their mistakes in their implementation.
